What is A/B Experimentation?

From the fundamental problem of causal inference to why randomization is the most powerful tool a data scientist has.

Series DS Interview Prep
Level Foundational + Rigorous
Companies Meta · Google · Airbnb · Netflix · Uber
The core question

Causation, not correlation

Most analytical tools tell you what happened. A/B testing tells you why. It answers one question with unusual precision: if I change X, does it cause a meaningful change in Y?

This matters because correlation is everywhere and almost always misleading. Users who see a red button may have higher CTR than those who see a blue one, but that could simply be because red-button users happen to be more engaged. A/B testing eliminates that ambiguity through randomization.

"Pre/post analysis conflates time effects with treatment effects. A/B testing holds time constant: the only systematic difference between groups is the thing you changed."

The math behind it

The fundamental problem of causal inference

For any user i, you can only ever observe one outcome: either what happened when they got the treatment, or what happened when they did not. Never both. This makes the individual treatment effect permanently unobservable:

Individual Treatment Effect (unobservable) ITE = Y(1) - Y(0)   ← can never observe both

What we can estimate is the Average Treatment Effect, the expected difference in outcomes across the whole population:

Average Treatment Effect (what A/B tests estimate) ATE = E[Y(1)] - E[Y(0)]

Randomization makes this possible. When users are randomly assigned, the control group's average outcome is a valid estimate of what the treatment group would have experienced without treatment:

Why randomization works E[Y(0) | Treatment group] = E[Y(0) | Control group]
Control group = valid counterfactual for treatment group
What must be true

Key assumptions

01
SUTVA
No interference between users. No hidden treatment variations.
02
Positivity
Every user has non-zero probability of being in either arm.
03
Randomization
Assignment is independent of user characteristics.
04
No peeking
Analysis only at pre-specified time. Not when results look good.
05
Stable population
Users during the experiment represent the target population.

SUTVA deserves special attention. It breaks constantly in real products, and when it does, your ATE estimate is biased downward (dilution bias). The true effect is larger than what you measured.

MetaTreatment user posts more, which changes content seen by control users in their network
AirbnbDiscounts to treatment guests reduce listing availability for control guests
UberTreatment rider surge pulls drivers away from control riders

Knowing the limits

When not to run an A/B test

Knowing when not to experiment is as important as knowing how. This signals mature thinking in interviews.

Low traffic Network effects Ethical constraints Irreversible changes Latent outcomes New market launch Tiny population

In each case, always follow up with an alternative: causal inference, difference-in-differences, synthetic control. The ability to pivot to the right method is the mark of a senior DS.

Interview traps

What interviewers are really testing

  • "It hit p<0.05 on Day 2. Ship it?"
    No. Peeking problem: p-values are only valid at the pre-specified analysis time. Two days also misses weekly seasonality and novelty effects.
  • "55% traffic went to control instead of 50%. What do you do?"
    Sample Ratio Mismatch. Stop analysis immediately. Results are invalid. Investigate logging bugs, hash collisions, bot filtering, and ramp artifacts.
  • "Clicks up, revenue flat. Ship?"
    No. Click quality likely degraded or cannibalization occurred. Revenue is the guardrail that matters.
  • "We cannot A/B test this, so we have no options."
    Wrong. Use causal inference alternatives: diff-in-diff, synthetic control, quasi-experiments.
  • "The iOS subgroup showed a big lift. Ship to iOS?"
    Depends. Pre-specified? Trustworthy. Post-hoc? Multiple comparisons risk. Treat as hypothesis and run a confirmatory experiment.
